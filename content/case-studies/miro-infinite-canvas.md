When the founders behind Miro started building what was then called RealtimeBoard in 2011, the idea of a shared digital whiteboard was a niche curiosity. Most collaboration happened in person, around physical whiteboards covered in sticky notes, or in documents and slide decks that one person edited at a time. The team, working out of Russia and later relocating its center of gravity westward, believed that visual, spatial collaboration, the kind designers, product teams, and facilitators did on walls and whiteboards, deserved a digital home that worked across distance. But for the better part of a decade, this was a hard sell. Companies were colocated, video conferencing was clunky, and the pain of not being able to brainstorm visually with a remote teammate simply was not acute enough for most organizations to pay for a dedicated tool. Miro grew steadily but unspectacularly, building a product for a future that had not yet arrived.

The problem Miro was solving was real but latent. Knowledge work increasingly involved distributed contributors, contractors, offshore teams, partners across cities, yet the most generative kinds of collaboration, workshops, brainstorms, journey mapping, retrospectives, remained stubbornly physical. When a key participant was remote, they were relegated to a grainy video square while the real thinking happened on a whiteboard they could not touch. Documents and slides could capture conclusions but not the messy, spatial, real-time process of arriving at them. The gap was a true multiplayer surface where dozens of people could think together visually, in the same space, at the same time, regardless of location. The catch was that demand for solving this problem was almost entirely theoretical until something forced the world to work apart.

The key decision was to invest patiently and deeply in the hardest parts of the product long before the market rewarded it. Building an infinite, zoomable canvas where many people edit simultaneously is a serious engineering challenge: it requires solving real-time synchronization, conflict resolution when users edit the same area, smooth performance as boards grow to thousands of objects, and reliable rendering across browsers and devices. Many competitors offered lighter-weight whiteboards that buckled under real collaborative load. Miro chose to treat the multiplayer infrastructure as the core product and poured years into making it robust, fast, and genuinely real-time. It paired this with a large, growing library of templates, brainstorming frameworks, agile boards, retrospectives, customer-journey maps, that lowered the blank-canvas anxiety and showed teams concrete ways to use the surface. This was an unglamorous, compounding bet on depth over flash.

Execution then met its moment in March 2020, when the COVID-19 pandemic forced knowledge workers worldwide to go remote almost overnight. Suddenly the latent pain became urgent and universal: teams that had always whiteboarded in person had no way to do it, and the in-person workshop, the design sprint, the planning session all had to move online. Miro was the most mature product ready to absorb that demand, because it had already spent nearly a decade building the hard infrastructure and the template ecosystem that made visual collaboration immediately usable. Adoption exploded as teams discovered that Miro could replace not just the whiteboard but entire categories of in-person collaboration. Its freemium model let teams start instantly and spread the tool organically within organizations, while paid plans monetized the expanding usage through a classic land-and-expand motion.

The results were dramatic. Miro's user base surged into the tens of millions, and the company raised a large round in early 2022 that valued it at roughly seventeen and a half billion dollars, an extraordinary leap for a product that had spent most of its life as a patient, slow-growing bet. It became the default collaborative-whiteboard tool for distributed teams across design, product, engineering, and consulting, embedding itself into the daily rituals, standups, retrospectives, planning, of remote and hybrid organizations. The years of unrewarded investment in real-time infrastructure paid off precisely because, when demand finally arrived, Miro did not have to build the hard part under pressure; it already had it.

The ripple effects accelerated the entire visual-collaboration category. Competitors and incumbents rushed in, with FigJam from Figma, Microsoft's Whiteboard, and others chasing the surge, validating that the collaborative canvas was now a core part of the modern work stack rather than a niche. As the world settled into hybrid work, the question shifted from whether teams needed an online whiteboard to which one would become the system of record for visual collaboration. Miro's challenge evolved into deepening into enterprise, expanding beyond whiteboarding into broader workflows, and defending its lead against well-funded entrants now that the category it had quietly cultivated was suddenly contested.

For product managers, Miro offers several lessons. First, some of the most valuable products are built for a future that arrives on its own schedule; investing in genuine technical depth before demand peaks means you are ready when the wave hits, rather than scrambling to build the hard part under fire. Second, infrastructure can be a moat, real-time multiplayer at scale is difficult enough that years of patient engineering created a durable advantage competitors could not quickly replicate. Third, lowering the activation barrier matters as much as the underlying capability; Miro's template library turned an intimidating blank canvas into immediate, concrete value. Finally, Miro illustrates that timing is often exogenous: you cannot manufacture a pandemic-scale shift, but you can position your product so that when the latent need becomes urgent, you are the obvious answer.
