In the late 1990s, the internet was a wild frontier, and e-commerce was severely handicapped by a fundamental problem: how do ordinary people send money to each other online securely and instantly? Traditional banks were slow and complicated, and mailing checks was archaic. A startup named Confinity, co-founded by Peter Thiel and Max Levchin, launched a product called PayPal to solve this. Originally designed to beam money between Palm Pilots via infrared ports, they quickly pivoted to focus on email-based payments on the World Wide Web. However, they faced a massive "cold start" problem. A payment network is useless if no one else is on it. They needed a massive user base, and they needed it yesterday, before well-funded banking incumbents could wake up and crush them.

Their solution was not traditional advertising. Banner ads and PR campaigns were expensive and yielded low conversion rates. Instead, the PayPal team decided to execute one of the most aggressive, audacious, and expensive growth hacks in the history of the internet: they decided to simply bribe people to use their product.

In late 1999, PayPal launched a brutally simple referral program. If you signed up for a new account, they deposited $20 directly into your PayPal wallet. If you referred a friend and they signed up, you got another $20, and your friend got $20. It was pure, unadulterated cash incentive. In an era before digital wallets were common, giving away free, immediately usable digital cash was an irresistible value proposition. 

The results were explosive and immediate. The promotion went viral across early internet forums and email chains. Growth hit an astonishing 7 to 10 percent *per day*. However, this hyper-growth was terrifying from a financial perspective. PayPal was burning through venture capital at an alarming rate, giving away millions of dollars to acquire users. Critics and traditional financial analysts thought the strategy was suicidal. They argued that PayPal was just buying disloyal users who would take the $20 and never use the service again. 

But Thiel, Levchin, and their team (which soon included Elon Musk after a merger with X.com) understood something fundamental about platform economics that their critics missed: the absolute primacy of network effects in a two-sided marketplace. In the payments business, the value of the network increases exponentially with every new user. By essentially paying a $20 Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), they weren't just buying a single user; they were buying a node in a rapidly expanding, inescapable web. 

This network effect crystallized dramatically when the PayPal user base began intersecting with the booming online auction site, eBay. Buyers wanted to pay quickly, and sellers wanted to receive funds instantly without waiting for checks to clear. The "bribe" had created enough critical mass that eBay sellers started adding "PayPal Accepted Here" logos to their listings to attract buyers. This, in turn, forced more buyers to sign up for PayPal. The referral program had brilliantly weaponized eBay's massive organic traffic, essentially hijacking eBay's platform to build PayPal's monopoly. 

As the network density reached critical mass, the organic, non-incentivized growth took over. Users were no longer joining for the $20; they were joining because it was the only practical way to conduct transactions on the internet. With the network effect secured, PayPal gracefully tapered the incentive. The $20 dropped to $10, then to $5, and eventually to zero. The cost of acquisition dropped off a cliff while the lifetime value of the customer soared. 

The gamble paid off spectacularly. PayPal reached 1 million users by March 2000 and 5 million by the end of the summer. They successfully went public in early 2002 and were subsequently acquired by eBay later that year for $1.5 billion—a deal entirely forced by the sheer inevitability of PayPal's network dominance on eBay's own platform.

Looking back from 2026, the PayPal $20 referral program is widely studied as the genesis of modern growth hacking. Today, PayPal is a behemoth, processing trillions of dollars annually, having expanded into everything from crypto to AI-driven consumer credit. In an era where startups endlessly optimize micro-conversions and run complex, opaque ad campaigns, the original PayPal playbook remains a stark reminder: sometimes, the most effective way to build an insurmountable, trillion-dollar network effect is to have the absolute conviction to simply buy your first million users with cold, hard cash.
