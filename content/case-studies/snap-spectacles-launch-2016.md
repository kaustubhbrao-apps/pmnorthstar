In the fall of 2016, Snap Inc. rebranded itself as a "camera company" and dropped a masterclass in guerrilla marketing. Rather than a standard Silicon Valley keynote, Snap introduced "Spectacles"—sunglasses with a built-in camera to record first-person video—via mysterious, smiling yellow vending machines called Snapbots. These bots would appear unannounced in random locations like Venice Beach or the Grand Canyon, sell a limited number of glasses, and disappear. The artificial scarcity generated a frenzy. Lines wrapped around blocks, and the $130 glasses began fetching upwards of $1,000 on eBay. For a brief moment, Snap had made wearable tech incredibly cool.

The marketing was flawless, but the hardware execution was fundamentally flawed. Blinded by the viral success and massive waitlists, Snap’s leadership assumed the demand was deep and sustained. They aggressively ramped up their supply chain, ordering massive quantities of components and fully assembling hundreds of thousands of units. However, they drastically misunderstood the nature of hype. The early adopters were buying the glasses for the novelty and the cultural cachet of owning an exclusive drop, not because the product solved a persistent daily need.

When Snap finally made Spectacles available for purchase online to the general public in early 2017, the bubble burst. The novelty had worn off. Furthermore, the product retention was abysmal; internal data leaked that less than half of buyers kept using the glasses after the first month. The friction of transferring videos to a phone, combined with the limited use case of circular, 10-second videos, meant the glasses ended up forgotten in desk drawers. Snap was left sitting on a mountain of inventory, forcing the company to take a brutal $39.9 million write-off on unsold hardware.

Many critics assumed this spectacular failure would permanently end Snap’s hardware ambitions. Instead, CEO Evan Spiegel viewed the $40 million write-off as an expensive but necessary tuition fee for learning the hardware business. Snap refused to abandon the form factor. They realized that their ultimate vision—overlaying digital reality onto the physical world—required them to control the hardware, lest they remain entirely at the mercy of Apple and Google's mobile operating systems. They took the Spectacles project back to the lab, shifting from consumer hype cycles to quiet, relentless R&D.

Looking at the landscape in 2026, Snap’s stubborn persistence has been completely vindicated. While competitors chased bulky, isolating VR headsets, Snap spent a decade refining lightweight optics. The launch of the Spectacles 5 in 2026—featuring fully untethered, agentic AI integration and seamless AR overlays in a frame that looks like standard Ray-Bans—has made them the dark horse winner in the spatial computing race. The 2016 launch is now viewed not as a failure, but as the critical, painful first step in a ten-year marathon to build the post-smartphone future.
