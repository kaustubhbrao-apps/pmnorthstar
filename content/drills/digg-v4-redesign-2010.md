## What actually happened — the reveal

This drill is based on the infamous launch of **Digg v4 in August 2010**. At the time, Digg
was a massive cultural force, but it was struggling to monetize and facing stiff competition
from an uglier but highly functional site called Reddit.

Under pressure to generate revenue and appease publishers, Digg's leadership pushed through
the v4 redesign. They removed the "bury" button (which users used to downvote spam), shifted
power from organic users to auto-posting publisher RSS feeds, and fundamentally altered the
site's democratic nature.

The backlash was instant and catastrophic. Power users revolted, literally flooding the Digg
front page with links to Reddit. Digg's leadership initially stayed the course, refusing to
roll back the changes. The result was the "Great Digg Exodus." Traffic plummeted, and Reddit
absorbed millions of Digg's disgruntled users, permanently flipping the power dynamic in
social news. 

Digg never recovered. Once valued at over $160 million, its core assets were sold in 2012
to Betaworks for a mere $500,000. It remains one of the most textbook examples in internet
history of a company destroying its own product by alienating its core community.
